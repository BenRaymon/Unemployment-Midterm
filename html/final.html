Bias in the Workplace: Unemployment¶

Diversity and inclusion in the workplace is an important topic in todays day and age. Many businesses attempt to provide a diverse and inclusive work environment for their staff, however, the attempts are not always so successful. One of the factors that impact diversity in the workplace is bias, specifically implicit or unconscious bias (Impact Group). Implicit bias is not based conscious decisions, but subconscious ideas that can influence a decision. For example, a common type of implicit bias is the affinity bias which states that people are more likely to associate with others who share similar traits. As people tend to like other people similar to them, Affinity bias can decrease diversity in the workplace. Affinity bias isn't the only implicit bias that can be seen in the workplace. Studies such as this one discuss the prevalence of agism in the workplace. Another study based on survey data indicates that white men are favored in the workplace compared to women or men of a different race.

While implicit biases are seen most commonly in the hiring stage, bias can be carried over to firing employees as well. By analyzing unemployment data I can determine the types of bias, if any, are prevalent in the stage of termination.

To determine if there is bias present in the workplace the following questions can be asked:

  1. Is there a difference in unemployment rate across races?
  2. Is the unemployment rate higher among younger or older age groups?
  3. What about gender? Are women unemployed at a higher rate than men?

It is important to consider the ethical impacts of analyzing data. The results of my analysis could be used to tailor discrimination laws in regards to termination. Federal anti-discrimination laws are already in place to prevent employers from discriminating on the basis of race, gender, or age. However, policy makers can tweak current policies or write new ones in an additional attempt to prevent possible discrimination that may be found during this analysis.

Some of the direct stakeholders would include employers and their employees. Employers may take these results into consideration when terminating their next employee, and the employees are directly impacted by the possibility of unemployment. The direct stakeholders hold similar values as both employees and employers should value diversity in the workplace. Indirect stakeholders would include policy makers, government, and the families of employees. Statistical Testing¶

The Mann-Whitney rank test is an appropriate non-parametric test for this independent data. The null hypothesis in every test will be that the sample mean is equal to the population mean. H0: $\mu$race = $\mu$total

The first set of tests will be conducted with an alternative hypothesis that the sample mean is not equal to the population mean: Ha: $\mu$race != $\mu$total

All four of the tests above resulted in a p-value that is less than 0.05. Because the p-value is so small in every test, all four alternative hypotheses can be accepted. The average unemployment rates among each race group are statistically significantly different from the total average unemployment rate.

The previous tests were conducted with a simple alternative hypothesis that $\mu$race != $\mu$total. Additional statisical tests can also be conducted with more specific alternative hypotheses based on the means calculated above.

  • Ha: $\mu$white < $\mu$total
  • Ha: $\mu$asian < $\mu$total
  • Ha: $\mu$black > $\mu$total
  • Ha: $\mu$hispanic > $\mu$total

Statistical Testing¶

The Mann-Whitney rank test is an appropriate non-parametric test for this independent data. The null hypothesis will be that the two means are equivalent. H0: $\mu$young = $\mu$old. Given the values of the two means, the alternative hypothesis will test if the young unemployment rate is significantly higher than the old unemployment rate. Ha: $\mu$young > $\mu$old

The alternative hypothesis is accepted as the p-value is < 0.05. It is accurate to claim that younger people (specifically 16-24 years old) have a significantly higher unemployment rate than older people (25+).

Statistical Testing¶

This data follows the same format as the previous sets of data. Similar to the other statistical tests, the Mann-Whitney rank test will be an appropriate non-parametric test for the independent data. The null hypothesis will be that the two means are equivalent. H0: $\mu$m = $\mu$w. Since the two means are so close together, the most appropriate alternative hypothesis would be that the two means are significantly different. Ha: $\mu$m != $\mu$w

In [154]:
print("p =", stats.mannwhitneyu(rate['Men'], rate['Women']).pvalue, " > 0.05")

p = 0.08142846141146426  > 0.05

Since the resulting p value is greater than 0.05, the alternative hypothesis fails to be accepted and the null hypothesis is held. It is accurate to state that the average unemployment rate for men and women are statistically the same. There is not enough evidence to claim that the two sample means are statistically different.

Conclusion¶

When breaking down unemployment rates among the 4 racial groups provided, there is adequate evidence that the total average is statistically different from the average among these groups. The Black or African American and Hispanic or Latino groups displayed unfavorable results compared to the total average. The unemployment rates in these categories are significantly higher than the average unemployment rate. On the other hand, the White and Asian categories both have unemployment rates that are significantly lower than the average. These results conclude that a higher portion of Black and Hispanic people are unemployed than White and Asian people, which correlates to a racial bias favoring the latter. While the analysis has shown that there is a significant difference in unemployment rates among these groups, further testing in the future can provide a more accurate representation to the effect of such differences.

Through an analysis of unemployment rates by age, it has been concluded that people in the younger age group tend to be unemployed at a significantly higher rate than older people. People between the ages of 16 and 24 are more than twice as likely to be unemployed than someone who is 25 or older. A study conducted by Sanna Malinen displayed a negative implicit bias against older people. Additionally, the study showed an explicit positive bias towards older people. One can hypothesize that when it comes to terminating employees, an explicit positive bias may outweigh a negative implicit bias. Another possible hypothesis is that the biases that are present during the hiring stage aren't as prevalent in the termination stage. It is important to note that the data used in this part of the analysis had a different range from the data in the rest of the analysis. The time period of the rate dataset ranges from 2001 up to 2021, yet the age breakdown of unemployment rates was only available from Feb 2020 to Feb 2021. The small sample size in this analysis causes the accuracy of these results to be questioned in comparison to results from data with a sample size of over 200.

The final observations made were based on unemployment rates between men and women. Surprisingly, there was no significant difference between the unemployment rate for men and the unemployment rate for women. While multiple sources, such as Berke and Forbes, argue the prevalence of sexism in the workplace, such sexism is not seen when it comes to the terminnation of an employee.

Based on these results, it is reasonable to conclude that a racial bias is present in the workplace. This racial bias has a negative impact on Black or Hispanic people while favoring White and Asian people. There is also an age bias favoring people that are 25 or older, however, the small sample size weakens the strength of these claims. Finally, no significant difference was shown in unemployment data among genders leading to the conclusion that a gender bias is not prevelant when it comes to terminating employees. Employers and policy makers can reflect on this analysis in an attempt to eliminate such biases from occuring.
